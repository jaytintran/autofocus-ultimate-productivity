# Autofocus AF5: The Productivity System That Actually Works

## What Is This?

Autofocus is a productivity app built around a simple truth: **your brain doesn't work like a prioritized to-do list**.

Most task management systems fail because they force you to rank everything by importance, then expect you to robotically work through that ranking. The result? Resistance, procrastination, and a constant feeling that you're working on the wrong things.

Autofocus works differently. It balances two parts of your mind:
- Your **rational mind** captures everything (the list)
- Your **intuitive mind** decides what's ready to be done (the "standing out" feeling)

Neither part runs the show alone. The result is less friction, less stress, more output, and focus that feels genuinely aligned with what matters.

---

## How It Works

### The Core System

1. **Write everything down** in one long list — no sorting, no ranking, no priorities. New tasks always go at the end.

2. **Work through the list page by page** (12 tasks per page), starting from page 1.

3. **On each page**, read through all tasks first without acting. Then ask: *what do I want to do?*

4. **When a task stands out** — feels ready, interesting, or simply right — work on it for as long as feels natural.

5. **When you stop**: if done, mark it complete. If not, re-enter it at the end of the list and mark the original complete.

6. **Continue scanning** the same page from where you left off.

7. **When nothing stands out**, move to the next page.

8. **After finishing the last page**, return to the first page that still has active tasks.

### What "Standing Out" Actually Means

Standing out isn't dramatic. It's subtle — a slight pull, a readiness, a sense that *now* is the right time for this particular thing. It might be curiosity, momentum from earlier work, or simply that the task feels less heavy than the others.

If nothing stands out, that's valid information too. Don't force it. Move to the next page.

## The Real Power: Long-Form Work

AF5 reveals its true strength with tasks that take days, weeks, or months to finish.

Consider: **"Finish the book Psycho-Cybernetics"**

- You work on it today, get through two chapters, stop naturally
- You mark it complete and re-enter it at the end of the list
- Tomorrow it comes back around
- You work on it again, another session, another re-entry
- Eventually you finish the book

What's left behind in your Completed Tasks view is a **trail**: every session you spent on that book, each one a completed task. The system doesn't just track that you finished the book — it shows you the work that went into it.

This is where most productivity systems break down. They're built for quick wins, not sustained effort. Autofocus is built for both.

## How to Write Tasks That Work

How you write a task changes how your brain responds to it. The same piece of work written two different ways produces two different levels of resistance.

### The Formula

```
Finish + [specific deliverable] + [enough context to be unambiguous]
```

### Examples

❌ Read the book Psycho-Cybernetics  
✅ **Finish the book Psycho-Cybernetics**

❌ Watch the negotiation course  
✅ **Finish Module 2 of the negotiation course**

❌ Work on the portfolio website  
✅ **Finish the homepage of the portfolio**

❌ Do the quarterly report  
✅ **Finish the Q3 section of the quarterly report**

Your brain responds to clear end states. A task that tells you what the world looks like when it's done is easier to start, easier to finish, and easier to re-enter when incomplete.

## The Tag System: How to Approach Work

Tags aren't just labels — they tell you how to *approach* a task before you even start it.

### 🎯 Finish
**Outcome-driven work with a clear completion state.**

You know exactly what "done" looks like before you start. These re-enter perfectly because the intent survives every cycle.

Examples:
- Finish the book Psycho-Cybernetics
- Finish the homepage of the portfolio
- Finish Module 3 of the negotiation course
- Finish the Q3 section of the quarterly report

**Re-entry: Medium** — Deliverable was too large. Break into smaller pieces.

### 🧭 Explore
**Intentionally open-ended work where the output is clarity, not a deliverable.**

You go in not knowing what you'll find. The task is to emerge with something more concrete — which then becomes the next 🎯 Finish task.

Examples:
- Explore ideas for the new app feature
- Research standing desk options
- Work on the content strategy direction
- Look into visa requirements for Japan

**Re-entry: High** — Expected. Each re-entry should produce new clarity or new Finish tasks.

### ⚡ Quick
**Single-shot tasks that take one sitting, have no re-entry scenario.**

Low friction, high throughput. If it takes under 15 minutes and you'll never need to re-enter it, it's a Quick.

Examples:
- Read the Stratechery piece on Apple
- Reply to Marco's email about the meeting
- Pay the electricity bill
- Watch the 8-minute video on cold outreach

**Re-entry: Low** — If a Quick re-enters, reclassify as Handle or Finish.

### 🔧 Handle
**Still one-shot but with more administrative weight.**

"Wash the dishes" is Quick. "Renew the car insurance" looks quick but often has steps, waiting, and follow-up — that's Handle territory.

Examples:
- Handle passport renewal
- Handle the car insurance renewal
- Handle the broken kitchen drawer
- Handle the Supabase billing issue

**Re-entry: Medium** — External dependencies and waiting periods. Re-entry is part of the process until resolution.

### How Tags Flow in Practice

A task often moves through types as it matures:

```
🧭 Explore content strategy direction
   ↓ you emerge with clarity
🎯 Finish the three-pillar content framework doc
   ↓ along the way you need to
⚡ Read the Wes Kao newsletter on positioning
```

- Reading list organized by domain (Fiction, Non-Fiction, Technical, Business, etc.)
- Reading progress tracking (page numbers and percentages)
- Status management (To Read, Reading, Completed, Paused, DNF)
- Notes and key takeaways capture

### Schedule View (Time Blocking)
- Create time blocks as containers for different parts of your day
- Drag-and-drop task scheduling
- Visual timeline from 6 AM to midnight with live current-time indicator
- Overlapping blocks support (e.g., "Exercise" + "Podcast" simultaneously)
- Mobile-optimized with tap-to-schedule flow

**Key insight**: A time block is not a task — it's a labeled container for a period of time. "Deep Work 9-11 AM" is the block. "Finish Chapter 3" is the task inside it.

## How This Will Change Your Life

### 1. You'll Stop Fighting Yourself

Most productivity systems create an adversarial relationship between you and your task list. You're constantly negotiating with yourself about what you "should" be doing versus what you actually want to do.

Autofocus eliminates that fight. When a task stands out, you work on it. When nothing stands out, you move on. No guilt, no forcing, no internal negotiation.

### 2. You'll Actually Finish Long-Form Work

Books you've been meaning to read for years? Courses you bought but never completed? Projects that keep getting pushed back?

The re-entry mechanism makes sustained effort feel natural. You don't need to finish the entire book today. You just need to work on it until it feels right to stop, then re-enter it. Tomorrow it comes back around.

### 3. You'll Build a Record of Real Work

Your Completed Tasks view becomes a trail of everything you've actually done. Not what you planned to do, not what you thought about doing — what you actually did.

For long-form tasks, you see every session. For quick tasks, you see the throughput. For exploratory work, you see the progression from vague to concrete.

This isn't just satisfying — it's informative. You learn what you actually spend time on, what kinds of tasks you avoid, and what patterns emerge in your work.

### 4. You'll Stop Abandoning Tasks Dishonestly

Most task lists become graveyards of things you'll never do but can't bring yourself to delete.

Autofocus surfaces this honestly. If a task keeps getting re-entered and never stands out, that's information. Either it's too vague (break it down), or it doesn't actually matter (delete it honestly).

The system doesn't let you hide from that reality.

### 5. You'll Work With Your Brain, Not Against It

Your intuition knows things your rational mind doesn't. It knows when you're ready for deep work versus admin tasks. It knows when you need to explore versus execute. It knows when a task is actually important versus just urgent-looking.

Autofocus gives your intuition a voice in the process. The result is work that feels aligned, not forced.

## Why This System Works When Others Don't

Most productivity systems fail for one of three reasons:

1. **They're too rigid** — forcing you to follow a structure that doesn't match how your brain actually works
2. **They're too complex** — requiring constant maintenance, categorization, and decision-making
3. **They're optimized for quick wins** — great for clearing small tasks, terrible for sustained effort

Autofocus solves all three:

- **Flexible structure** — your intuition guides what to work on, but the list keeps you honest
- **Minimal overhead** — add tasks at the end, work on what stands out, re-enter if incomplete
- **Built for long-form work** — the re-entry mechanism makes sustained effort feel natural

The system doesn't try to optimize your productivity. It tries to align your work with how your brain actually functions. The productivity is a side effect.

## Getting Started

1. Add everything you need to do to the list — no sorting, no ranking
2. Start on page 1 and scan through the tasks
3. When something stands out, work on it
4. When you stop, mark it complete or re-enter it
5. Continue until nothing stands out, then move to the next page
6. Trust the process

The system works if you let it. Don't force priorities. Don't skip ahead. Don't try to optimize it. Just follow the steps and see what happens.

## The Bottom Line

Autofocus isn't a productivity hack. It's not a life optimization system. It's not trying to make you superhuman.

It's a tool that helps you work with your brain instead of against it. It helps you finish what you start. It helps you see what you've actually done. It helps you stop lying to yourself about what matters.

If you're tired of fighting your task list, if you're tired of abandoning long-form work, if you're tired of productivity systems that create more stress than they solve — try this.

One list. One task. Trust the process.
